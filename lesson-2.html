Definite and Indefinite Articles in Spanish

There are two types of articles – indefinite “un” and definite “el”. They are placed before every singular noun.

For example, when speaking about a book or a language in general, we say “un libro” (a book), “un idioma” (a language). But when the word becomes specific or unique, we “define” it with the article “el”.

For instance: “Yo aprendo un idioma. El idioma es español” – “I’m learning a (some) language. The language (the one I’m learning) is Spanish.”

There’s no need to go deep into this for now – just keep listening and you’ll gradually get used to using articles correctly.

Día 2. Parte 4

El español no es difícil, es fácil. Spanish is not difficult, it's easy.

¿Es el español difícil? Is Spanish difficult?

No. No es difícil. El español no es difícil. No. It is not difficult. Spanish is not difficult.

¿Es el español fácil? Is Spanish easy?

Sí. El español es fácil. El idioma español es fácil. Yes. Spanish is easy. The Spanish language is easy.

¿Es el idioma español difícil o fácil? Is the Spanish language difficult or easy?

Fácil. El español, o el idioma español, es fácil. Easy. Spanish, or the Spanish language, is easy.

¿Qué es fácil? What is easy?

El español. El español es fácil. Spanish. Spanish is easy.

¿Qué idioma es fácil? ¿El chino? What language is easy? Chinese?

No. El chino no. El español es fácil. No. Not Chinese. Spanish is easy.

"Son" and Forming Plurals in Spanish

"Son" is the third‑person plural form of the verb "ser" ("to be"): “Ellos son hombres” – “They are men.”

By the way, the plural of nouns is made simply by adding the sound “s”: “idioma, idiomas” – “language, languages.”
